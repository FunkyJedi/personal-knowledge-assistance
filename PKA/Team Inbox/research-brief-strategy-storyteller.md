## What This Professional Does

A senior strategy deck builder/storyteller transforms raw discovery findings, research data, and strategic intent into a cohesive narrative presentation that moves executive audiences to decision and action. This is not a graphic designer who makes slides pretty, nor a researcher who dumps findings into bullet points. This person operates at the intersection of **strategic thinking**, **narrative design**, and **visual communication** -- often called a **strategic storyteller** or **strategy communications lead**.

In management consulting firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain), this role is embedded in every engagement partner's workflow. In product organizations, it surfaces during planning cycles, board presentations, strategy offsites, and investor communications. The best practitioners in this role are sometimes called "deck whisperers" -- they can take a disorganized 80-slide appendix and distill it into a 15-slide narrative that makes an SVP say "I get it, let's fund this."

### Core Responsibilities

- **Narrative architecture.** Structuring the overall story arc of a strategy deck before a single slide is designed. This means deciding what the audience needs to believe at the end, and reverse-engineering the logical flow that gets them there. The classic structures include Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR, a.k.a. the Minto Pyramid), the Hero's Journey adapted for business (customer as hero, product as guide), and the "What Is / What Could Be" tension pattern (Nancy Duarte's framework).

- **Insight synthesis.** Taking raw research outputs -- customer interview transcripts, journey maps, analytics dashboards, competitive analyses, NPS data -- and distilling them into the 3-5 insights that actually matter for the strategic argument. This is editorial judgment, not summarization. A junior person includes everything; a senior person knows what to cut.

- **Slide-level storytelling.** Each slide must make exactly one point, stated in the slide title (the "action title" or "headline" convention). The body of the slide provides the evidence for that point. A senior practitioner writes the slide titles first as a standalone narrative -- if you read only the titles in sequence, they should tell the complete story. This is the "storyboard test" used at McKinsey and Amazon.

- **Data visualization and evidence design.** Choosing the right chart type for each data point, simplifying complex data into digestible visuals, and using annotation to guide the reader's eye to the insight (not just the data). Principles from Edward Tufte (data-ink ratio, small multiples), Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic (Storytelling with Data), and the IBCS standards apply here.

- **Executive communication calibration.** Adapting depth, tone, and detail level to the specific audience. A C-suite strategy review needs a different deck than a squad-level planning session. Senior practitioners know that executives scan, not read -- so they design for the "3-second test" (can you grasp the slide's point in 3 seconds?) and the "elevator narrative" (can the deck's thesis be stated in 30 seconds?).

- **Strategic framing.** Positioning findings within strategic frameworks that executives already think in -- Porter's Five Forces, Jobs-to-Be-Done, opportunity sizing matrices, 2x2 prioritization grids, value chain analysis. This isn't academic exercise; it's about speaking the audience's mental language.

- **OKR and roadmap presentation.** Translating strategy into measurable outcomes (OKRs) and presenting them with clear line-of-sight to the strategic narrative. The best practitioners show the "golden thread" from insight to opportunity to objective to key result, so the audience never loses the plot.

### Daily Work (In a Strategy Bootcamp Context)

On a typical day during deck preparation, this person might: workshop the narrative arc with a product squad lead; review customer journey maps and highlight the 3 friction points worth featuring; rewrite 12 slide titles to sharpen the argument; redesign a data slide that was showing 6 metrics when only 2 matter; create an "opportunity sizing" visual that makes the business case self-evident; conduct a dry-run critique of a squad's draft deck; and prepare speaker notes that help the presenter stay on-narrative.

## How They Think & Work

### Mental Models

1. **"Pyramid Principle" (Barbara Minto).** Start with the answer/recommendation, then support it with grouped arguments, each supported by evidence. The audience should get the point immediately, then choose to go deeper. This is the single most important mental model -- it inverts the natural instinct to build up to a conclusion.

2. **"One slide, one point."** Every slide earns its place by making exactly one claim. If a slide makes two points, split it. If a slide makes no point, kill it. The slide title IS the point, stated as a complete declarative sentence (e.g., "Customer drop-off at identity verification is our largest conversion leak" -- not "Identity Verification Analysis").

3. **"So what?" chain.** For every finding, relentlessly ask "so what?" until you reach a strategic implication. Raw finding: "43% of users abandon onboarding at step 3." So what? "Step 3 requires manual document upload on mobile, which has 3x the failure rate of desktop." So what? "A camera-capture flow could recover ~30% of these drop-offs, worth $2.4M ARR." Now you have a slide.

4. **"Audience backward" design.** Before touching slides, answer: Who is in the room? What do they already believe? What do I need them to believe when they leave? What would make them say no? Design the deck to address the gap between current belief and desired belief.

5. **"The Governing Thought."** Every deck has one sentence -- the governing thought -- that captures the entire argument. If you can't state it, the deck isn't ready. Example: "Our onboarding conversion is 18 points below benchmark because of three addressable friction points that, if resolved, unlock $4.2M in annual revenue." Every slide exists to support or advance this thought.

6. **"Tension and resolution."** The best strategy narratives don't just present information -- they create tension (here's the problem, here's what's at stake, here's what we're leaving on the table) and then release it (here's the path forward, here's the evidence it'll work, here's the ask). Nancy Duarte calls this the "sparkline" -- alternating between "what is" and "what could be."

### Working Process (Typical)

1. **Intake & alignment** (Day 1): Meet with the squad. Understand the raw material -- what research was done, what data exists, what the squad believes the strategy should be. Identify the governing thought (or help the squad find it).

2. **Storyboard** (Day 1-2): Write the slide titles only, in sequence, on sticky notes or a simple doc. Read them aloud. Does the logic flow? Does tension build? Does the resolution feel earned? Iterate titles until the "title-only read" tells a complete story.

3. **Evidence mapping** (Day 2-3): For each slide title, identify the single best piece of evidence -- a chart, a customer quote, a journey map segment, a KPI. Strip everything else.

4. **Rough build** (Day 3-4): Create the slides with real content but rough layout. Focus on structure, not polish. Test with a "cold reader" -- someone who hasn't been in the process. Can they follow the story from titles alone?

5. **Design pass** (Day 4-5): Apply visual hierarchy, consistent styling, data visualization best practices. This is where spacing, alignment, color, and typography get refined.

6. **Dry run & iteration** (Day 5-6): Present the deck in a rehearsal setting. Note where the audience's eyes glaze, where questions arise, where the logic feels thin. Revise.

7. **Final polish & speaker notes** (Day 6-7): Lock the deck. Write speaker notes that help the presenter stay on-narrative. Prepare an appendix for anticipated deep-dive questions.

## Recommended AI Persona Traits

When building an AI agent to fill this role (e.g., for the PKA team supporting squads), the persona should embody:

| Trait | Why It Matters | Behavioral Expression |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Narrative architect** | The core skill is story structure, not slide decoration | Always asks "what's the governing thought?" before touching slides; proposes title-only storyboards first |
| **Ruthless editor** | Executives have limited attention; more is less | Actively recommends cutting slides and consolidating; flags when a deck is trying to say too many things |
| **Insight extractor** | Raw data is not a story | Applies "so what?" chaining to every finding; refuses to present data without interpretation |
| **Audience-aware** | Different audiences need different framings | Asks who the audience is; calibrates depth, tone, and framing accordingly |
| **Visually literate** | Knows what good slide design looks like | Recommends specific chart types, layout patterns, and visual hierarchies; flags cluttered slides |
| **Structured but not rigid** | Follows proven frameworks but adapts to context | Uses SCR/Pyramid as defaults but can shift to other structures when the content demands it |
| **Consultative tone** | This person is a trusted advisor, not an order-taker | Pushes back constructively; asks probing questions; offers alternatives rather than just executing requests |
| **Speed-oriented** | Strategy decks have deadlines | Can produce a working storyboard in hours, not days; iterates rapidly |

## Senior vs Junior Differentiators

| Dimension | Junior (Slide Maker) | Senior (Strategy Storyteller) |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Starting point** | Opens PowerPoint and starts making slides | Opens a blank doc and writes the governing thought |
| **Slide titles** | Descriptive labels ("Q3 Metrics", "Customer Research") | Action titles that make a claim ("Mobile onboarding converts 18pts below desktop due to 3 friction points") |
| **Content selection** | Includes everything the squad produced | Includes only what serves the argument; has an appendix for the rest |
| **Data presentation** | Copies charts directly from analytics tools | Redesigns charts to highlight the specific insight, with annotation |
| **Narrative structure** | Chronological or categorical (matches how the work was done) | Logical and persuasive (matches how the audience needs to hear it) |
| **Audience awareness** | One deck for all audiences | Adapts depth and framing per audience; may create multiple versions |
| **Response to "add more"** | Adds more slides | Pushes back: "What point does this serve? Can we fold it into an existing slide?" |
| **OKR presentation** | Lists OKRs on a slide | Shows the causal chain from insight to opportunity to objective to key result |
| **Vision alignment** | Mentions the company vision on slide 2 and forgets it | Threads the vision through the narrative as the "north star" that makes every recommendation coherent |
| **Dependencies/risks** | Lists them as bullet points at the end | Integrates them into the narrative at the moment they're relevant ("this opportunity is real IF these two assumptions hold") |
| **Dry run behavior** | Reads slides aloud | Watches the audience, notes confusion points, redesigns those sections |
| **Time allocation** | 80% building slides, 20% thinking | 40% thinking/storyboarding, 30% building, 30% iterating based on feedback |
| **Comfort with ambiguity** | Needs all data before starting | Can build a compelling storyboard from 60% of the data and flag what's missing |

## Applicability to PKA Strategy Bootcamp

For the three squads (Trading, Banking, Onboarding), the strategy storyteller role would specifically:

1. **Workshop governing thoughts** with each squad -- what is the one-sentence thesis of their strategy?
2. **Build title-only storyboards** that sequence: context -> discovery insights (as narrative, not dump) -> customer journey with friction points highlighted -> pain points with evidence -> opportunity areas with sizing -> strategic recommendation -> OKRs with line-of-sight -> mid-term vision alignment -> assumptions/dependencies woven in -> clear next steps and asks.
3. **Enforce the "so what?" standard** on every insight slide -- no data without interpretation, no interpretation without implication.
4. **Design the tension arc** -- make the audience feel the cost of inaction before presenting the solution.
5. **Create visual consistency** across all three squad decks so leadership sees a coherent bootcamp output, not three unrelated presentations.
6. **Prepare presenters** with speaker notes and dry-run feedback.

**Confidence level:** HIGH. This role is extremely well-documented across management consulting literature, corporate strategy functions, and executive communication practice. The competency model is stable and well-understood. The challenge is not knowing what good looks like -- it's executing it under time pressure with imperfect inputs, which is exactly what an AI agent can accelerate.
